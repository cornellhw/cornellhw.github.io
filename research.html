Illuminating Change: Evaluating Photovoltaic Poverty Alleviation's Economic Impact in China
with Hao Yu and Yunwei Li, Lijinyu Liu
Prepare to submit: Ecological Economics, Forthcoming.

This study delves into the impact of photovoltaic (PV) poverty alleviation initiatives in rural China from 2000 to 2019, using a comprehensive dataset of approximately 41,426 data points from various levels of government. Our analysis is structured around four pivotal hypotheses. Hypothesis 1 posits that PV poverty alleviation increases rural per capita disposable income by creating additional income channels for farmers. Hypothesis 2 suggests a threefold environmental benefit: (1) PV power plants reduce carbon emissions by replacing coal power, enhancing the green productivity of agriculture; (2) these plants contribute to green productivity by facilitating agricultural mechanization; and (3) they further improve agricultural green productivity through increased crop yields. Hypothesis 3 explores the broader economic impact, asserting that PV poverty alleviation as an industrial policy generates positive externalities, stimulating economic development in surrounding regions. Finally, Hypothesis 4 examines the differential effects of various PV plant policies. To evaluate these hypotheses, we employ a time-varying Difference-in-Differences (DID) approach coupled with a fixed-effect model. Our findings illuminate the multifaceted role of renewable energy policies in rural economic development, highlighting how PV initiatives not only contribute to environmental sustainability but also foster significant economic benefits.
